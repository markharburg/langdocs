A 200 Gbps per Lane Driver for Micro-Ring Modulator

Zhubiao Zhu, Marco Fiorentino, Chaerin Hong

Hewlett Packard Labs

{zhubiao.zhu, marco.fiorentino, chaerin.hong}@hpe.com

 

Abstract

The ever-increasing data transmission driven by emerging applications has fueled the demand for faster and more efficient optical interconnects. As an example, there will be increasing pressure on Slingshot to increase bandwidth per wavelength over the next several generations.  HPE and others have developed 200 Gbps PAM4 micro-ring modulators(MRM) devices.  But to our best knowledge, no such CMOS MRM driver has yet been reported to support 200 Gbps system.  Here we report on the novel design of such high-speed driver.  Simulations show that that the proposed design works at 200 Gbps PAM4/7/13 and 250 Gbps PAM13.   We invented a 3-state driver which improves high voltage protection, enables high PAM signaling, and lowers power consumption.  We introduced the Bessel filter to the MRM driver design which is the first time application to that special design case for bandwidth extension, , where it's the best leverage for the tri-state driver. We found ways to improve MRM’s bandwidth dependence on bias voltage.  The higher-order PAM driver has big advantages on low power and high-speed and also reduces design challenges for clocking, pre-driver and digital signal processing. This proposed driver circuit design provides a way to meet need. HPE can license this technology to Slingshot suppliers to upgrade bandwidth for our products. This solution is part of a larger investigation into the use of photonic links for photonics scale-up networks targeted at clusters of GPUs.

Problem statement

The surge in data transmission driven by emerging applications such as data centers, cloud computing, extended reality (XR), and generative artificial intelligence (AI) has heightened the demand for faster and more efficient optical interconnects. There will be increasing pressure on Slingshot to increase bandwidth per wavelength over the next several generations.   High-speed Micro-ring Modulators (MRMs) developed by HPE and others have achieved data rates up to 200 Gbps with PAM4 modulation [1-6]. However, no 200 Gbps CMOS MRM driver has been published yet [7-9]. The highest data rate driver currently available (112 Gbps) suffers from excessive power consumption.

 

Traditionally, the MRM-based links face challenges related to high-power consumption and data rate limitations. One primary issue is the requirement for a voltage swing larger than 2 V for the CMOS driver to drive MRM. The state-of-the-art CMOS circuitry implemented with 28nm, or larger process nodes consumes large power, which results in poor energy efficiency. Another significant challenge is induced by the bandwidth limitation of MRM because of its strong dependence on the bias voltage.

 

 

Our solution

Figure 1.  Illustration of A. 2-state PAM4, B. 3-state PAMn and C. The driving stage circuit.

·       Current Limitations: All published drivers for MRMs use a 2-state driver mechanism (pull-up and pull-down). Differential PAM4, however, requires four voltage levels: -4, -2, 2, and 4. The voltage level 2 (like -2) is achieved as shown in Figure 1A: LSBt = -1, MSBt = 2, LSBb = 1, and MSBb = -2. This mixed pull-up and pull-down state creates a “driver fight,” reducing the voltage margin for high voltage protection in a 1-segment MRM. 

·       3-state driver: We developed a 3-state driver by adding a tri-state (both pull-up and pull-down are off). As illustrated in Figure 1B, PAM13/7/4/2 can be easily obtained without any driver fight, thus improving high voltage protection. This technique allows us to use low process nodes like TSMC 7 nm (we used this to do design and simulation) and 3 nm to design high voltage swing MRMs. Low process nodes contribute to both high speed and low power consumption. In this state, the driver pulls to VDD, consuming no DC current, thereby saving power. The higher-order PAM reduces clock frequency and power consumption significantly, making circuit design much easier.

·       Bandwidth Extension: Figure 1C shows the driver with four added inductors (La, Lb, Lc, and Ld). This circuit drives one of the four pads for the 2-segment MRM, which is composed of anode and cathode for each segment. The inductor and capacitor values at drv1a, drv1b, drv1c, drv1d, and pad1 are chosen to form a Bessel filter for bandwidth extension.  The capacitor values are proportional to the driving slice driving strength.  Bessel filters are the best for high-speed electrical SerDes to address high ESD capacitors (connected to drv1c and drv1d nodes) required for electrical SerDes. The MRM driver does not have large ESD devices, and we connect the driving slices to drv1b/c/d nodes to further extend the bandwidth. To increase the voltage swing, we also tested operating the FETs in the linear region, and the Bessel filter aids in this, representing a new application of the Bessel filter.

·       Feed-Forward Equalization (FFE): While FFE is commonly used in high-speed electrical and optical SerDes circuits (e.g., HPE’s 7 nm 106 Gbps electrical SerDes chip) to boost overall bandwidth, we opted not to use FFE to reduce power consumption. Instead, we employ the above-described techniques (tri-state, increased PAM levels, and Bessel filter) to design a high-speed, low-power driver for MRMs.

·       Bandwidth Management: The bandwidth of MRMs is highly dependent on the bias voltage, leading to varying transient behaviors at different signal levels. To address this, we use a slower input signal for the top circuits’ (MSBt and LSBt in Figure 1B) pull-up and the bottom circuits’ (MSBb and LSBb) pull-down to balance the bandwidth differences. With adjustable capacitor values at p12* and n12* (in fig 1C) can also be used.   Additionally, we create distinct Bessel filters for the pull-up and pull-down paths to further optimize performance.

 

When FFE is introduced, the effective main‐cursor amplitude (or “main tap swing”) can be reduced compared to a simple non‐emphasized driver because part of the channel compensation is being handled by carefully boosting or attenuating neighboring symbols. Typical reductions in the main swing might be 20%–40% which can be save by the special Bessel filter of our design with FFE. Without FFE reduces TX power about 10%–25% for 112 Gbps PAM4 in 7 nm process. Tri-state driver increase the driver protection and so increase the swing by about 0.3V.

 

With the increased swing of this technology. We can design MRM drives with lower process node like 7nm and 3nm. This helps to reduce power. For example, for 128 Gbps PAM4, the power is decreased by about 30% and 50% for MRM based Serdes using 7nm and 3 nm instead of 28 nm TSMC process nodes. Using lower process nodes make it possible for higher bit rate Serdes.

 

Evidence the solution works

The MRM used here is a 2-segment MRM developed and modeled by HPE [4] and its Verilog-A model is equipped with the proposed transmitter design for simulations discussed in the following sections.  We have results for 1-segment MRM which will be reported in the future.

Figure 2. A. AC sweep simulation result with pad Capacitance = 0 fF (A) and 50 fF (B) at various MRM bias voltages 1, 1.5, 2, 2.5, and 3V.

Figure 2 illustrates a key characteristic of MRMs—their bandwidth is strongly influenced by the bias voltage.

 

As the bias voltage decreases, so does the bandwidth.  For the 50 fF pad capacitor case, the bandwidth decreased from 30.4 GHz to 13.3 GHz when the bias voltage decreased from 3 V to 1 V when the Bessel filter is not applied. The red lines represent cases without the Bessel filter, while the cyan lines show cases with the Bessel filter. The clear distinction between these lines demonstrates the effectiveness of the Bessel filter in enhancing bandwidth.

 

In Figure 3, the “CFR” denotes the data Clock Frequency Ratio relative to PAM4 at 200 Gbps.  From PAM4 to PAM13, the clocking frequency is reduced to 0.31 of the frequency of PAM4 under same 200 Gbps condition.  This is the big advantage for power performance and circuit design.  The “eSW” represents the electrical signal swing to the MRMs.  We obtained a 2.7 V swing for some cases which are operating at linear operating region of the FETs. For the MRM HPE designed [4], the electrical swing from CMOS requirement is only 1.6 V.  All the simulated results shown here meet this requirement.  The final item listed in the diagrams indicates the power efficiency of the driver (excluding the pre-driver) under these conditions. For better comparison, all y-axes range is set to 0.4, and all x-axes are proportional to time, shown in 1UI.  As it is shown, the power efficiency is higher with higher PAM.  This circuit is mixed signal type and has DC current consumption for some cases.  The power efficiency with lower-order PAM has stronger advantage for digital circuits like pre-driver, clocking and receiver side digital signal processing which is power hungry for high-speed Serdes including receiver for this MRM system. 

 

The diagrams in Figure 3 reveal that noise is more pronounced at lower PAM operations, such as PAM4, because the signal operates at a higher frequency range where transmission functions are less efficient.   The higher-PAM levels, like PAM13, offer significant advantages in terms of lower power consumption and reduced circuit design challenges. We have successfully fabricated electrical SerDes chip operating at 106 Gbps PAM4. When designing for 200 Gbps using PAM13, the pre-driver design timing requirements are equivalent to those of PAM4 at 61.5 Gbps, reducing clocking and pre-driving circuit design challenges. This also aids the receiver side in handling multiple eyes. Lower clocking circuits result in significantly reduced power consumption for both the driver and receiver.  For digital circuits, the power consumption is proportional to the clocking frequency.  At 200 Gbps PAM4, the circuits become more complex for strict timing requirements.  For example, each clocking buffer needs an inductor.  High-speed circuit’s power consumption is more than just proportional to the clocking frequency. It has been shown that silicon photonic microring modulator operating at up to 330 Gb/s with PAM-8 with driving voltage under 2 Vp and meets soft-decision forward-error correction specifications [11].  They stated that PAM-8 is a better solution than PAM-4 for high data rate.

Competitive approaches

Intel has developed a 112 Gbps PAM4 driver for MRM [8]. They stated that the power is too high [7].  The data rate is low compared with our design 250 Gbps PAM13, and 200 Gbps PAM13/7/4.    

Current status

By the Techcon 2025, we complete the investigation phase of the key driving circuit for a possible chip design for a 200 Gbps MRM based system for HPE.  The results are very exciting.  The high PAM (PAM13 and PAM7) design is excellent on high-speed and low power.  This solution is part of a larger investigation into the use of photonic links for photonics scale-up networks targeted at clusters of GPUs. Combining the high-speed links enabled by microring modulators and dedicated photonics architectures could lead to a significant competitive advantage for HPE’s HPC/AI solutions.

Next steps

We are continuing to refine our designs and the photonic devices models to improve the accuracy of our transceiver simulations.  We are also planning to develop a design for the receiver side of the link to conduct a full-link simulation.   Finally, we are looking for funding to tape out a proof-of-concept chip in a TSMC 7 nm shuttle run.
